.308 Reloading Guide
Dad's step-by-step reference
📋 Overview

The Full Process

Every reload follows the same order. Tap any step to see details.

1
Sort & Inspect
Check brass for cracks or damage
PREP ▼

Look at every case. Throw out any with cracks in the neck, splits on the body, or a bright ring near the base (that means the brass is about to split). Better to lose one case than ruin your rifle.

✅ Good brass: smooth, no cracks, no bright rings near the base.
2
Deprime
Punch out old primer first
PREP ▼

Do this before cleaning. Mount the decapping die in your press and run each case through to punch out the spent primer. With the pocket empty, the ultrasonic cleaner can reach inside and clean it — saving you the brush step.

✅ Deprime first = ultrasonic does all the cleaning, inside pocket and out.
3
Clean
Ultrasound bath — pocket cleaned too
PREP ▼

Run deprimed cases in your ultrasonic cleaner with diluted brass solution for 30–45 minutes. Because the primer pocket is already open, the ultrasonic waves clean inside it — no brush needed. Dry completely before moving on. Moisture near primers is dangerous.

✅ Cases bright inside and out. If any pockets still have black carbon, a quick 5-second brush fixes it.
4
Brush Pocket & Deburr Flash Hole
Carbon out of pocket — remove machining burr from flash hole
PREP ▼

① Pocket Brush — use the large rifle brush. Insert and rotate several times until all black carbon is gone. The ultrasonic bath will have done most of this already — you're just finishing any stubborn spots.

② Flash Hole Deburr — on new, unfired brass only. A small conical cutter on a long shaft is inserted from the case mouth all the way down to the flash hole. It removes the machining burr left on the inside face of the case head where the flash hole was pierced during manufacture. A sharp burr can deflect the primer's flash unevenly, causing ignition inconsistency. One or two turns is all it takes — once done, never again on that piece of brass.

✅ Flash hole deburr is a one-time operation on new brass. Brush the pocket every firing. Takes about 10 seconds per case.
⚠️ Military brass often has a crimped primer pocket — the crimp must be swaged or reamed out before you can seat a new primer. You'll feel it when the primer won't go in.
5
Anneal Case Necks
Restore brass softness — prevents neck cracking
PREP ▼

Restore brass softness — prevents neck cracking and ensures consistent neck tension. Cases spin on their axis in the flame for even heat. Only the neck and shoulder get hot — base stays cool (critical for primer pocket integrity).

✅ Correct anneal: neck turns straw/light gold colour. Over-annealed: blue/purple (too hot). Under: no colour change.
⚠️ Cases spin on their axis in the flame for even heat. Only the neck and shoulder get hot — base stays cool (critical for primer pocket integrity).
6
Lube Cases
Prevents case from sticking in die
PREP ▼

Roll each case lightly on the lube pad. Cover the body of the case — do not get lube on the shoulder or you'll get dents. A little goes a long way. Without lube, the case will stick inside the die.

⚠️ Too much lube = shoulder dents. Too little = stuck case. A light even coat is all you need.
7
Shoulder Bump
Body die only — set shoulder back 0.001"–0.002"
RESIZE ▼

Set shoulder back 0.001"–0.002" for reliable chambering — body die only, neck untouched. The body die contacts only the case body and shoulder — the neck passes through the open cavity untouched.

✅ Use a headspace comparator gauge. Bump only what's needed — over-working brass shortens case life.
⚠️ Body die does NOT size the neck. Neck tension is set separately in the next step.
8
Neck Size
Neck die only — sets bullet tension
RESIZE ▼

Size the neck only — sets neck tension that holds the bullet firmly. The bushing controls how much the neck is squeezed. Keeping neck sizing separate from body sizing preserves case body dimensions and extends brass life significantly.

✅ Bushing neck dies let you dial exact neck tension by choosing bushing size. Most precision shooters use 0.001"–0.002" neck tension.
⚠️ Keeping neck sizing separate preserves case body dimensions and extends brass life significantly.
9
Trim to Length
Cases stretch — must be trimmed to 2.005"
PREP ▼

Brass grows longer every time it's fired. After sizing, measure every case from base to mouth with your digital calipers. The target is 2.005". If any case is at or over 2.015", trim it.

How to use the calipers:

  • Close the jaws fully and press ZERO (or ORIGIN) on the display
  • Open jaws, place the case base against the fixed jaw
  • Slide the moving jaw gently to the case mouth — don't force it
  • Read the number. 2.005"–2.010" = good. At or over 2.015" = trim it.
  • After trimming, measure again to confirm you hit 2.005"
⚠️ Cases too long can jam into the rifling and cause dangerous pressure spikes. Always measure — don't guess.
10
Deburr & Chamfer
Smooth the case mouth
PREP ▼

After trimming, the case mouth has a sharp edge inside and out. Use the deburring tool — a few turns outside to chamfer, a few turns inside to deburr. Just until smooth — don't over-do it.

✅ A properly chamfered case mouth lets the bullet start straight without shaving lead or copper.
11
Prime
Seat new primer slightly below flush
⚠ CRITICAL ▼

Use a hand priming tool (Lee, RCBS, or Frankford Arsenal). Feel for resistance as it seats — a slight "crush fit" is correct. The primer face must be 0.002"–0.005" below flush with the case base. Never above.

⚠️ A HIGH primer (sticking up) can fire the moment the bolt closes — before you even pull the trigger. Always check by feel and visually inspect every primer.
✅ Correct feel: smooth firm resistance, primer slightly recessed. Wrong feel: drops in with no resistance = discard that case.
12
Charge Powder
Weigh every charge on a scale
⚠ CRITICAL ▼

Use your reloading manual to find the correct charge for your bullet. Good beginner powder for .308: Varget. Weigh every single charge on your scale. Keep only ONE powder on the bench at a time — label it clearly.

⚠️ After charging cases, look across all of them in the loading block. All powder levels should look the same height. An empty case or overfull case is a double-charge or missed charge — stop and investigate.
✅ Always start at the MINIMUM load listed in your manual. Never use maximum to start.
13
Seat Bullet
Press bullet to correct depth
LOAD ▼

Set your seating die to the correct depth using your reloading manual. The finished round should measure 2.800" overall length (OAL) for most .308 loads. Measure every few rounds with calipers to stay consistent.

✅ Seat 3 rounds, measure each, then fine-tune the die before doing the rest of the batch.
14
Final Inspect
Plunk test + visual check every round
⚠ CRITICAL ▼

Drop each finished round into a case gauge (or the rifle chamber with bolt removed). It should fall in freely with the base flush or below the gauge face. If it binds or sticks — find out why before shooting that round.

⚠️ Also visually check all primers are seated below flush. Look for any rounds that look off — bent bullet, high primer, case dent.
✅ When done: label your box with caliber, bullet, powder, charge weight, primer, and date. Store in a cool dry place.
